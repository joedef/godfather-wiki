# Deleted & Restored Scenes

Roughly an hour of material was cut from the first two films and later restored, first for *[The Godfather Saga](recuts-and-restorations.md#the-godfather-saga-1977)* in 1977 and then in fuller form across the 1981, 1992, 2001, and 2008 home releases.

Two things are worth saying up front. First: **the cuts were almost all correct.** The theatrical *Godfather* is tighter and better than any expanded version of it, and the restored scenes mostly explain things the film was stronger for implying. Second: several of them are genuinely wonderful, and one of them — Genco's deathbed — is a real loss.

Below, the material that matters. This is not exhaustive; the various restorations disagree with each other about what's included and in what order.

---

## From *The Godfather* (1972)

### Genco Abbandando's deathbed

The most significant cut in the trilogy. In the novel, Vito's original consigliere **Genco Abbandando** — the man whose name is on the olive oil company — is dying of cancer on the day of Connie's wedding. Vito leaves the reception with his sons and goes to the hospital. Genco, terrified, begs the Don to stay with him and *use his power* to hold death off, as though Vito could make an offer to that too.

Vito cannot. The scene is the only moment in the film where the Godfather's authority is shown to have a hard edge — a thing it cannot buy, cannot threaten, cannot fix.

Losing it costs the theatrical cut two things: the reason Tom Hagen is consigliere at all (he's filling a vacancy, not holding a job), and an early statement of the film's actual subject. It's in every restored version.

### The Lucy Mancini material

The novel gives Sonny a mistress, **Lucy Mancini**, a bridesmaid at Connie's wedding; the film reduces their affair to one wordless scene upstairs. More was shot — including material connecting Lucy to Sonny's death and its aftermath — and cut.

Coppola was right to cut it and wrong to cut all of it: eighteen years later *Part III* has to announce that Sonny fathered [Vincent](../characters/vincent-mancini.md) on Lucy Mancini, which lands as an invention because the film never established her. See [Book vs. Film](../analysis/book-vs-film.md).

### Michael in Sicily, after the bomb

The theatrical film leaves Michael's exile after Apollonia's death almost entirely to a cut. Restored footage extends the aftermath — Michael's recovery, the hunt for the traitor Fabrizio, and more of Don Tommasino — which changes the pacing of the return without improving it.

### More Sonny

Several Sonny scenes were trimmed in post because they didn't advance plot, including additional material around the Corleone-Tattaglia war and Sonny's conduct as acting Don. [James Caan](../people/james-caan.md)'s performance loses shading; the film loses nothing.

### Vito's homecoming

An extended sequence around Vito's return from the hospital, including more of the family reassembling around him, and more of Clemenza and Tessio in the house.

### Assorted

- Longer Luca Brasi rehearsal and delivery of his speech
- Extended Connie/Carlo domestic scenes before the beating
- Additional Michael and Kay material in the New Hampshire and hotel scenes
- More of the Corleone bodyguards and the mattresses period

## From *Part II* (1974)

### Vito's business, expanded

The restored versions give substantially more of Vito's rise between the Fanucci killing and the 1925 Sicily trip: the growth of Genco Pura, his early accommodations with other neighborhood figures, and how the protection racket he never calls a protection racket actually functioned day to day.

This material is more valuable than the *Godfather* restorations, because *Part II*'s theatrical Vito timeline is a highlight reel — four or five perfect scenes with the connective tissue removed. Restored, it's a business story.

### Young Sonny

Additional scenes with **young Santino**, including his witnessing of things a child should not witness and his first steps into the life. It sets up the man who dies at a tollbooth better than the theatrical cut does. Ed Falco's [*The Family Corleone*](../books/the-family-corleone-2012.md) later expanded this territory into a whole novel.

### Extended Cuba

More of the Havana sequence: the American executives, the Batista apparatus, and the sense of a system in its last week. The theatrical cut is already the most historically specific stretch of the trilogy; the restored version is more so.

### The Corleone family, before

Small domestic scenes of Vito and Carmela's household in the 1920s, Michael as a boy, and the family's move from the tenement.

## What was never shot

Some famous "deleted scenes" don't exist:

- **Brando in the 1941 flashback.** The ending of *Part II* was written for Vito to arrive on camera. Brando did not appear — accounts differ on whether this was a fee dispute or a refusal — and the scene was rewritten so that everyone rushes off to greet a father the audience never sees. It is better as written around him.
- **A fourth film.** No footage exists. See [The Unmade Part IV](unmade-part-iv.md).
- **Tom Hagen in Part III.** Duvall never shot a frame. See [Part III § The Duvall problem](the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md#the-duvall-problem).
