The strangest artifact in the franchise: an adaptation of an official *Godfather* sequel novel from which every trace of *The Godfather* had to be legally removed before it could be released.

---

## The problem

Puzo's 1984 novel [*The Sicilian*](../books/the-sicilian-1984.md) is framed as an interlude inside *The Godfather*: Michael Corleone, in his second year of Sicilian exile in 1950, is given one last job before he can go home — escort the bandit **Salvatore Giuliano** to America. The Giuliano story is the book; Michael is the frame.

Puzo's novel rights and Paramount's film rights to the *Godfather* characters were separate properties. When producers took *The Sicilian* to screen without Paramount, Michael Corleone came off the table — along with Clemenza and every other Corleone-adjacent element.

So the adaptation keeps the bandit and throws away the frame. What's left is a Salvatore Giuliano film with no connection to the saga at all, released with a title that promises one.

---

## Plot

Sicily, 1943–1950. **Salvatore Giuliano** (Christopher Lambert), a young man from Montelepre, kills a *carabiniere* who catches him smuggling grain and takes to the mountains. He becomes a bandit and then something larger — redistributing seized land and grain to the peasantry, commanding genuine popular loyalty, and demanding Sicilian independence.

He is courted and used by everyone: the Mafia boss **Don Croce Malo** (Joss Ackland), the separatist movement, the landowning aristocracy, and the Christian Democrats in Rome. His cousin and lieutenant **Aspanu Pisciotta** (John Turturro) is turned against him. In 1950 he is shot dead, and the state announces a version of events that satisfies no one.

The real Giuliano's story ended the same way, and it remains one of the great unresolved cases in modern Italian history. See [Real-World Mafia](../reference/real-world-mafia.md#salvatore-giuliano).

## Production and reception

Michael Cimino was, in 1987, seven years past *Heaven's Gate* and still radioactive. *The Sicilian* was a European co-production with a difficult shoot and a familiar Cimino ending: the producers cut roughly half an hour, releasing a 115-minute version, and Cimino disowned it. A 146-minute director's cut later appeared on home video and is the only version worth seeking out — it's a slower, more coherent, considerably better film, and still not a good one.

Christopher Lambert — French-American, a year past *Highlander* — as a Sicilian folk hero was the casting decision that most reviewers led with. Terence Stamp, as an aristocratic prince, and John Turturro, in an early role, come off best.

It was a commercial and critical failure. It also drew a lawsuit from **Gore Vidal**, who had written a draft of the screenplay and disputed the credited attribution to Steve Shagan; the Writers Guild arbitrated in Shagan's favor.

---

## Is it canon?

No, in any useful sense. Without Michael it has no connection to the Corleones; with Michael restored, the novel it's based on still sits uneasily against the 1972 film, which accounts for those same two years very differently. See [Continuity](../continuity.md#michaels-sicilian-exile).

For the Giuliano story itself, **Francesco Rosi's *Salvatore Giuliano* (1962)** is the film to watch — a formally radical Italian docudrama that Martin Scorsese has repeatedly named among his favorite films, and which is superior to Cimino's version in every respect.
