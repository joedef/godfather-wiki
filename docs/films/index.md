## What the three films are each about

They are not three chapters of one story. They are three different arguments.

**The Godfather (1972) is about succession.** It's a coronation story told as a horror film: a young man who has deliberately placed himself outside his family is drawn back in, one reasonable decision at a time, until he is the thing he refused to be. Its structure is a family gathering (the wedding), a crisis (the shooting), an exile, a return, and a second family gathering (the baptism) that mirrors the first and inverts it. Nothing in it is wasted.

**Part II (1974) is about cost.** It runs two stories at once — Vito building the family from nothing between 1901 and 1925, Michael dismantling it between 1958 and 1959 — and the cutting between them is the entire argument. Everything Vito gains, Michael loses in the adjacent scene. Where the first film asks *how does a man become a monster*, the sequel asks *what does the monster have left*. The answer is a chair, a lake, and nobody.

**Part III (1990) is about absolution, and whether it's available.** Michael has spent twenty years trying to buy his way clean, and the film's thesis is that the legitimate world he's trying to enter — the Vatican Bank, the international holding company — is more corrupt than the one he came from, and less honest about it. Its reputation is poor. Some of that is earned. A lot of it isn't. See [Part III Reassessed](../analysis/part-iii-reassessed.md).

## The recurring craft team

The trilogy's consistency across eighteen years is unusual, and it's mostly the same names:

| Role | I (1972) | II (1974) | III (1990) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Director | Francis Ford Coppola | Coppola | Coppola |
| Screenplay | Puzo & Coppola | Coppola & Puzo | Puzo & Coppola |
| Cinematography | Gordon Willis | Willis | Willis |
| Production design | Dean Tavoularis | Tavoularis | Tavoularis |
| Editing | William Reynolds, Peter Zinner | Zinner, Barry Malkin, Richard Marks | Malkin, Lisa Fruchtman, Walter Murch |
| Music | Nino Rota (Carmine Coppola addl.) | Rota & Carmine Coppola | Carmine Coppola |
| Casting | Fred Roos | Roos | Roos |

Rota died in 1979, which is why *Part III*'s score is Carmine Coppola's alone, working from Rota's themes. Willis retired from features in 1997; *Part III* was his last collaboration with Coppola. See [The Crew](../people/the-crew.md).
