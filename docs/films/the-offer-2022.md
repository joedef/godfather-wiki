A ten-part dramatization of how *The Godfather* got made, told from the point of view of its producer, **[Albert S. Ruddy](../people/albert-s-ruddy.md)** — a former computer programmer who had produced *Hogan's Heroes* and one cheap Robert Redford picture, and who talked his way into the most valuable property in Hollywood.

It arrived for the film's fiftieth anniversary. The reviews were mixed and the subject is irresistible, which describes the show fairly well.

---

## What it covers

The production war, essentially as recounted on [The Godfather (1972) § Production](the-godfather-1972.md#production), dramatized and compressed:

- Ruddy landing the job and immediately being in over his head
- Robert Evans running Paramount while fighting Gulf+Western's Charles Bluhdorn over budget
- Coppola being hired because he was cheap and Italian-American, then fighting for period, location, Brando, and Pacino
- The casting wars, especially the Brando screen test
- **[Joe Colombo](../reference/real-world-mafia.md#joe-colombo-and-the-league)** and the Italian-American Civil Rights League: the threats, the shot-out car windows, and Ruddy's direct negotiation
- Bettye McCartt, Ruddy's assistant, as the show's most consistently competent character
- The premiere, and the vindication

## Reception

**57% on Rotten Tomatoes**, with a consensus that it was overstuffed with unnecessary subplots. **48 on Metacritic.**

The specific complaint that recurs: the show has one great story — a producer caught between a studio that wants to fire his director and a crime family that wants to shut him down — and repeatedly leaves it to service a large ensemble it doesn't have time for. The parts that work are the ones with Colombo in them, largely because Giovanni Ribisi plays him as a man who genuinely believes his anti-defamation campaign is righteous, which is the most historically interesting thing about the real Colombo.

Matthew Goode's Robert Evans was the consensus highlight — a full-throttle impression of a man who was already a caricature of himself. Both Goode and Juno Temple drew **Critics' Choice** nominations; Temple won a **Satellite Award**. The series was nominated for Best Limited Series at the Critics' Choice Awards.

## How accurate is it?

Reasonably, on structure; loosely, on scenes.

The load-bearing events are documented: Paramount's reluctance, the near-firings, the Brando test, the League agreement, the budget fights. What's invented is most of the dialogue, several of the confrontations, and the tidiness — real production histories don't have act breaks.

The larger caveat is one the show can't escape: it is dramatizing an account given by its own protagonist. Ruddy told these stories for fifty years, and other participants — particularly Coppola and Evans, who spent decades disputing each other's versions of who saved the film — tell them differently. Mark Seal's *Leave the Gun, Take the Cannoli* (2021), which covers the same ground journalistically, is the better source and the one to read alongside.

Ruddy died in 2024, aged 94.
