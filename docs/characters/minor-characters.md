# Minor Characters

Everyone else worth knowing, alphabetically by first appearance within each group.

---

## The Corleone organization

**Genco Abbandando** *(Frank Sivero, young)* — Vito's boyhood friend, business partner, and first consigliere. The olive oil company carries his name. He dies of cancer on the day of [Connie](connie-corleone.md)'s wedding, begging Vito to use his power to hold death off. The scene was shot for the 1972 film and cut; it appears in the [restored versions](../films/deleted-scenes.md) and is the most significant deletion in the trilogy. His absence from the theatrical cut is why [Tom Hagen](tom-hagen.md)'s appointment as consigliere is never explained.

**Al Neri** *(Richard Bright)* — Michael's enforcer and eventual underboss. A former New York police officer, which is why he can put on a uniform and shoot [Barzini](barzini-and-the-five-families.md) on the courthouse steps. He is also the man in the boat with [Fredo](fredo-corleone.md). Neri is the closest thing Michael has to a [Luca Brasi](luca-brasi.md), and the fact that he was a cop is the film's driest joke about institutions. Bright appears in all three films.

**Rocco Lampone** *(Tom Rosqui)* — Recruited by Clemenza during the war; kills Paulie Gatto in the marshes; later a capo in Nevada. He shoots [Hyman Roth](hyman-roth.md) in the Miami airport and is killed within seconds — the only Corleone soldier we watch die on the job.

**Paulie Gatto** *(John Martino)* — Vito's driver, who called in sick the day of the shooting. Taken for a drive to the New Jersey marshes to "look at some apartments"; Clemenza stops for a piss, Rocco shoots him through the back of the head, and Clemenza delivers the cannoli line. **[Robert De Niro](../people/robert-de-niro.md) was originally cast in this part** and left for another film when Pacino's casting freed a role — which is how he was available to play young Vito three years later.

**Willi Cicci** *(Joe Spinell)* — A soldier in both films; in *Part II* he testifies before the Senate committee and delivers the exchange about never being given a direct order — *he never says nothin'… he just moves his finger.* Spinell later starred in *Maniac* (1980) and was a fixture of 1970s New York cinema.

**Fabrizio** *(Angelo Infanti)* — One of Michael's Sicilian bodyguards. Paid by Barzini's people, he plants the bomb that kills [Apollonia](apollonia-vitelli.md) and flees. The novel and the [restored cuts](../films/deleted-scenes.md) pursue his fate; the theatrical film simply lets him walk away, which is worse and better.

**Calò** *(Franco Citti)* — The other Sicilian bodyguard, and the only one who stays loyal. He reappears in *Part III*, forty years older, and kills [Don Lucchesi](zasa-altobello-lucchesi.md) with his own spectacles. Citti was a Pasolini regular — *Accattone*, *Oedipus Rex*.

**Don Tommasino** *(Corrado Gaipa, then Vittorio Duse)* — Vito's old friend in Sicily, who shelters Michael in exile and is crippled in the process. He returns in *Part III*, is murdered, and Michael delivers a eulogy over his body that is really about himself. In [*The Sicilian*](../books/the-sicilian-1984.md) he is a central figure.

**The Rosato brothers** *(Danny Aiello, Carmine Caridi)* — Bronx operators under [Hyman Roth](hyman-roth.md) who try to strangle [Pentangeli](frank-pentangeli.md) in a bar. Aiello improvised the line delivered while doing it — *Michael Corleone says hello* — which is why Pentangeli spends the rest of the film believing Michael ordered his death.

## The petitioners and the neighborhood

**Amerigo Bonasera** *(Salvatore Corsitto)* — The undertaker. The first face and first voice in the trilogy: *I believe in America.* He wants two men killed for beating his daughter and offers money; Vito, offended, extracts friendship instead. The favor is called in ninety minutes later, when Vito brings him [Sonny](sonny-corleone.md)'s body and asks him to use all his skill. The film's most perfectly closed loop.

**Nazorine the baker** *(Vito Scotti)* — Wants his daughter's fiancé, an Italian POW, kept in the country. Granted.

**Enzo the baker's assistant** *(Gabriele Torrei)* — The fiancé. He turns up at the hospital to pay respects to the Don and ends up standing on the steps beside Michael, bluffing away an assassination party with an empty hand in his coat. Afterward he cannot light his own cigarette. Michael can. It's the moment Michael learns what he is.

**Signor Roberto** *(Leopoldo Trieste)* — The landlord in *Part II* whom young Vito persuades, with escalating courtesy, to let a widow keep her apartment and her dog. A masterclass in how a favor economy is built.

**Don Fanucci** *(Gastone Moschin)* — The Black Hand extortionist in the white suit who taxes the neighborhood. Vito kills him during the Feast of San Rocco, and the entire Corleone family exists because of it. Fanucci is the trilogy's clearest picture of what came *before* organized crime got organized: a loud man with a knife and no structure.

**Don Ciccio** *(Giuseppe Sillato)* — The Sicilian chieftain who murdered Vito's father, brother, and mother in 1901. Vito returns in 1925, tells the blind old man his name, and cuts him open.

## Nevada, Washington, and the world

**Senator Pat Geary** *(G. D. Spradlin)* — Takes a bribe and insults Michael's family in the same conversation: *I don't like your kind of people… I despise your masquerade.* [Tom Hagen](tom-hagen.md) later frames him for a prostitute's death and owns him permanently. Geary reappears at the Senate hearing praising Italian-Americans, which is the joke.

**Johnny Ola** *(Dominic Chianese)* — Roth's fixer, and the man who turns [Fredo](fredo-corleone.md). Chianese later played Uncle Junior on *The Sopranos*, which makes his appearance here retroactively load-bearing for an entire genre.

**Deanna Dunn Corleone** *(Marianna Hill)* — Fredo's wife. Drunk, loud, and humiliating at the Communion party. Her existence is a two-minute summary of Fredo's marriage.

**Jack Woltz** *(John Marley)* — The Hollywood studio head who refuses [Johnny Fontane](johnny-fontane.md) a part and wakes up with a horse's head. Marley's scream is real; the head was real, sourced from a dog-food supplier.

**Anthony Corleone** *(Franc D'Ambrosio in Part III)* — Michael and Kay's son, who refuses law school for opera. His debut in *Cavalleria Rusticana* at the Teatro Massimo is the setting for the film's finale — a son singing while his father's enemies are murdered, which is the [baptism sequence](../analysis/baptism-sequence.md) restaged with the sacrament swapped for art.

**Mosca** *(Mario Donatone)* — The Sicilian assassin hired by Altobello. He kills [Mary](mary-corleone.md) with a bullet meant for Michael.

**B. J. Harrison** *(George Hamilton)* — The lawyer invented to replace [Tom Hagen](tom-hagen.md) after [Duvall's exit](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md#the-duvall-problem). Competent, and not a brother.

## The Coppolas

The trilogy is a family business in more ways than one:

| Person | Relationship | Appearance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Carmine Coppola** | Father | Composer (*Part II* and *III*); on screen as a piano player in the first film |
| **Italia Coppola** | Mother | Extra |
| **Talia Shire** | Sister | [Connie Corleone](connie-corleone.md) |
| **Sofia Coppola** | Daughter | Infant at the 1972 baptism; immigrant child in *Part II*; [Mary](mary-corleone.md) in *Part III* |
| **Roman Coppola** | Son | Child extra |
| **Gian-Carlo Coppola** | Son | Child extra |
| **Anton Coppola** | Uncle | Conductor, on screen in *Part III* |
| **Gia Coppola** | Granddaughter | Infant in *Part III* |

**Roger Corman** — Coppola's first employer — appears as a senator in *Part II*. **Harry Dean Stanton** plays an FBI agent. **Tony Sirico**, later Paulie Walnuts on *The Sopranos*, appears uncredited as a Rosato hood. **Troy Donahue**, a 1950s teen idol, plays Connie's fiancé and is named "Merle Johnson" — which was Troy Donahue's real name.
