---

## The character

Mary runs her father's charitable foundation, which is the laundering operation dressed as philanthropy, and she believes in it completely. She is the only person in [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md) with no idea what the family is.

She falls in love with her first cousin [Vincent](vincent-mancini.md). Michael and Kay both find it intolerable, for different reasons — Kay because it's incest, Michael because it's the business. He ends it as the price of Vincent's succession.

Then, on the steps of the opera house where her brother has just made his debut, the assassin Mosca fires at Michael. The first shot misses. The second hits Mary. She looks at her father, says *Dad?*, and dies.

Michael screams — and Coppola cuts the sound out entirely, so that the widest, ugliest gesture in the whole trilogy plays in silence before the audio comes back. It's the best shot in the film and one of the best in the series.

---

## Why she has to die

Structurally, Mary is the answer to a question the trilogy has been asking since 1972.

Every atrocity Michael commits is justified as protection of family. The films have already shown what that protection is worth: [Apollonia](apollonia-vitelli.md) blown up in a car meant for him, [Fredo](fredo-corleone.md) shot on a lake by his order, [Kay](kay-adams.md)'s pregnancy ended deliberately to stop another Corleone son existing. Mary is the last item on the bill. The man who destroyed everything he loved in order to protect it finally loses the one person he was protecting, to a bullet aimed at himself.

The rhyme with Apollonia is exact and intentional — same cause, same mechanism, thirty years apart. Michael's life is bracketed by two women killed by weapons meant for him.

## The casting

Mary was, in order: **Julia Roberts** (accepted, left for *Pretty Woman*), **Rebecca Schaeffer** (scheduled to audition; murdered by a stalker hours before the meeting), **Madonna** (tested; judged too old opposite García), **Winona Ryder** (cast, arrived in Rome, withdrew within days from exhaustion), and finally **Sofia Coppola**, the director's 18-year-old daughter, cast in an emergency with the production already three weeks behind.

She had appeared in both previous films — as the infant being baptized in 1972, and as an immigrant child in *Part II*.

---

## The reception, and what it cost

The performance is not good. Sofia Coppola was a non-actor placed among Actors Studio veterans and asked to carry the emotional load of a film's ending, and the tonal mismatch is real.

What happened next was disproportionate by any measure. She won two Razzies — Worst Supporting Actress and Worst New Star — and became the standing punchline for the film's failure, in a press campaign directed at a teenager. Her father has said the critics used his daughter to attack him and has never stopped being angry about it, and has noted the irony that a film about a man whose child pays for his sins produced exactly that outcome in life.

**Roger Ebert** defended her at the time, arguing that a polished professional would have brought a slickness the part is better off without, and that the girl's awkwardness suits a character who does not belong in this world.

She stopped acting. She directed *The Virgin Suicides* (1999), then *Lost in Translation* (2003), for which she won the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay and became the first American woman nominated for Best Director. Coppola has said the 2020 [*Coda*](../films/the-godfather-coda-2020.md) recut vindicates her performance; that's a father talking, but the re-edit does treat her more kindly.
