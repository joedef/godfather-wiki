Winegardner's second and final *Godfather* novel, and the most aggressive of the continuations: it kills a character the films left alive, and it drives the Corleone story straight into the Kennedy assassination.

---

## Plot

**1963–1964.** Michael Corleone is still trying to go legitimate and still living with what he did to [Fredo](../characters/fredo-corleone.md). The former capo **Nick Geraci** — Winegardner's invention, presumed dead at the end of [*The Godfather Returns*](the-godfather-returns-2004.md) — has spent years in hiding and emerges to take Michael apart methodically.

Geraci's campaign includes turning the Commission against Michael and killing **[Tom Hagen](../characters/tom-hagen.md)** in the Florida Everglades.

Running alongside it: the Shea brothers. **President Jimmy Shea** and his brother **Attorney General Daniel Shea** are transparent Kennedy analogues — the Attorney General waging a public war on organized crime while the family's debts to the mob go unpaid. Shea is assassinated by a Cuban national, in a killing the novel implies was arranged by New Orleans boss **Carlo Tramonti** — a plot built directly on the long-standing Carlos Marcello conspiracy theories.

Michael eventually traps and kills Geraci, then Tramonti and others. In the last pages, Connie reveals that Fredo left an illegitimate child.

---

## The problem with killing Tom Hagen

*Part III* establishes that Hagen died sometime before 1979, unexplained — a consequence of [Robert Duvall's salary dispute](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md#the-duvall-problem) rather than any story decision. Winegardner takes the opening and supplies a death.

Whether that's a service or a trespass depends on your tolerance for the continuation project generally. It is at minimum a *choice*: the films leave the most decent person in the story to disappear off-page, and the novel decides he was murdered in a swamp by a character who does not exist in the films.

---

## The Kennedy material

This is the most divisive thing about the book, and the most defensible.

Puzo's original novel is a book about how organized crime and legitimate American power are the same enterprise wearing different clothes. Winegardner's Shea plot pushes that thesis to its logical destination — the mob as a participant in the central American trauma of the century. It is not subtle, but it is not unfaithful either, and it draws on a body of real speculation (Marcello, Trafficante, Giancana, Judith Exner) that historians have taken seriously for sixty years. See [Real-World Mafia](../reference/real-world-mafia.md).

The objection is tonal: *The Godfather* works because it is claustrophobic and domestic, and a novel in which the Corleones brush against the Kennedy assassination is no longer that book.

---

## Reception and the lawsuit

Reviews were mixed and generally cooler than for *The Godfather Returns*. Sales were respectable but below the first.

More consequentially, the novel had been authorized by the **Puzo estate** but not by **Paramount Pictures**, which holds the film rights. That disagreement — over who may license new *Godfather* works, and in which media — festered and eventually erupted around Ed Falco's [*The Family Corleone*](the-family-corleone-2012.md) in 2012, with the studio and the estate suing each other. It settled in 2013.

Winegardner has not written another.
