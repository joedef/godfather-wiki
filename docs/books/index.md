# The Books

<p class="epigraph">Behind every great fortune there is a crime.
<cite>The epigraph Puzo attached to the novel, attributed to Balzac — see below</cite></p>

Five novels, written across forty-three years by three authors, of which one is a genuine cultural event and the rest are downstream of it.

## Reading order

**If you've seen the films:** read [*The Godfather*](the-godfather-1969.md). Stop there unless you want more.

**If you want more:** [*The Family Corleone*](the-family-corleone-2012.md) next, because it's a prequel and doesn't contradict much, then Winegardner's two in order. [*The Sicilian*](the-sicilian-1984.md) last — it's the least connected and the most demanding.

**If you have not seen the films:** watch the films first. The novel's best material is improved by the adaptation so consistently that reading first is a downgrade. See [Start Here](../start-here.md#should-i-read-the-book).

## What the novels have that the films don't

The 1969 novel is a substantially larger and messier object than the film cut from it. What Coppola left behind:

- **Vito's entire backstory**, told in flashback — the Sicilian childhood, the Fanucci murder, the olive oil business. Coppola cut it, then filmed most of it as half of [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md).
- **Johnny Fontane's Hollywood career**, a substantial subplot about a singer losing and rebuilding his voice, which Puzo treats seriously and the film reduces to two scenes.
- **Lucy Mancini and Jules Segal** — Sonny's mistress and the doctor she takes up with in Las Vegas. This storyline is the novel's most notorious material and the most reliable reason readers put it down.
- **The internal mechanics** of how the Families operate: the Commission, the accommodation with the police, the political payroll. Puzo explains; Coppola shows.
- **Michael's Sicilian exile** in detail, including the traitor Fabrizio's fate.

And the films have things the novel doesn't: the garden scene between Vito and Michael (written by Robert Towne), the ending of *Part II*, and everything in *Part III*.

Full comparison: [Book vs. Film](../analysis/book-vs-film.md).

## On that Balzac epigraph

Puzo's epigraph — *"Behind every great fortune there is a crime"* — is one of the most-quoted lines in the book, and Balzac did not write it.

The nearest real sentence is from *Le Père Goriot*: *le secret des grandes fortunes sans cause apparente est un crime oublié* — "the secret of great fortunes without apparent cause is a forgotten crime." The compression into the punchier version predates Puzo, but *The Godfather* is why everyone knows it.

The misquotation is fitting for the book. Puzo took a piece of received wisdom, sharpened it past accuracy, and made it permanent — which is roughly what he did to the American public's idea of the Mafia. See [Real-World Mafia](../reference/real-world-mafia.md#what-puzo-invented).
