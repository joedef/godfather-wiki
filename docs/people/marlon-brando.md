## Before

Omaha; an alcoholic mother who acted locally, a difficult father; expelled from military academy. He arrived in New York in the mid-1940s and studied with **Stella Adler**, whose approach — build the character from imagination and observation outward — he credited for the rest of his life while pointedly refusing to credit Lee Strasberg. *Strasberg never taught me acting. Stella did — and later Kazan.*

**A Streetcar Named Desire** on Broadway in 1947 changed American acting. Film work followed fast: *The Men*, the *Streetcar* film (1951), *Viva Zapata!*, *Julius Caesar*, *The Wild One*, and **On the Waterfront** (1954), which won him Best Actor and contains the taxi-cab scene that every subsequent generation of actors has been made to study.

Then a long, strange decline. *Mutiny on the Bounty* (1962) was a legendary production disaster and Brando took most of the blame. Through the 1960s he made films that lost money and acquired a reputation for being impossible: uninterested, unprepared, contemptuous. By 1970 Paramount's position was not that Brando was expensive. It was that he was **banned**.

## Casting

Puzo had written Vito imagining Brando and wrote to him directly. Paramount president Stanley Jaffe's response was reportedly categorical: as long as he was in charge, Brando would not be in this picture.

[Coppola](francis-ford-coppola.md) got around it by proposing a "makeup test" at Brando's house, ostensibly to test equipment. On camera, Brando — with no direction — put shoe polish in his hair, stuffed tissue in his cheeks, rolled his collar, and dropped his voice into a rasp built from listening to recordings of **Frank Costello's** televised Kefauver testimony. He became, in about ninety seconds and in complete silence, a sixty-year-old Sicilian.

Coppola put the tape in among the other auditions and let the executives find it. They approved him on three conditions: a low fee, a personal bond against overruns, and a screen test he had, technically, already passed.

He was paid a reported **$250,000** up front plus points. He sold the points back early. It is one of the worst financial decisions in the history of acting.

---

## The performance

The cotton wool in the screen test became a **dental prosthetic** for the shoot, which is why the jaw hangs.

Beyond that, almost everything about Vito is subtraction: he moves slowly, gestures rarely, and almost never raises his voice. [Gordon Willis](gordon-willis.md) lit him from above and let the eyes go into shadow — a decision Brando embraced, because it means the audience cannot read him and has to lean in.

The famous business was largely his: the cat in the opening scene was a stray found on the Paramount lot and put in his lap on the day; the orange peel in the mouth during the death scene was improvised to make his grandson laugh.

He was also, by every account, generous and mischievous on set — he pulled elaborate practical jokes, and he made a point of protecting Coppola when the firing rumors circulated.

The **cue cards** were real and lifelong. Brando's position was that memorizing lines produced a recitation, and that reading them fresh produced the hesitation of actual thought. Whatever you make of the theory, the results here are hard to argue with.

## The refusal

He won Best Actor and did not attend. **Sacheen Littlefeather** appeared in his place and declined the award on his behalf, citing the film industry's treatment of Native Americans and the ongoing standoff at Wounded Knee. She was given about sixty seconds; Brando's prepared statement ran to pages, and she read it publicly afterward. The room booed and applauded in roughly equal measure.

It remains the most famous political gesture in Academy history. The Academy formally **apologized to Littlefeather in 2022**, shortly before her death; after she died, her claimed Apache heritage was publicly disputed by her own sisters, and the matter has not been resolved.

Brando's own account is on [the Dick Cavett interview](../media/interviews.md#brando-and-the-oscar-refusal).

## After

*Last Tango in Paris* (1972) — released within months of *The Godfather*, and the source of a controversy over the filming of one scene that has permanently complicated his and Bertolucci's reputations. *Apocalypse Now* (1979) — he arrived enormously overweight and without having read the book, and Coppola shot him in shadow and rewrote around him. *Superman* (1978) — a reported $3.7 million for roughly twelve minutes, then a record. *A Dry White Season* (1989) earned a final nomination.

The last decades were largely tragedy: his son Christian's conviction for killing his daughter Cheyenne's partner in 1990, Cheyenne's suicide in 1995, deteriorating health, and financial trouble. He died on **July 1, 2004**.

His last recorded acting job was the voice of Vito Corleone for [the 2006 video game](../reference/video-games.md) — recordings largely unusable because of oxygen-equipment noise, with a sound-alike completing the part.
