# The Crew

The department heads and the people whose names are on the awards nobody quotes.

---

## Dean Tavoularis — Production Design

**All three films.** Academy Award for *Part II* (with Angelo Graham and set decorator George R. Nelson).

Tavoularis (b. 1932) is the trilogy's other great visual author, and the one who gets least credit because good production design is designed not to be noticed.

His single greatest achievement is *Part II*'s **1917 Little Italy** — several blocks of period New York built on East 6th Street in Manhattan, dressed to a degree of density that no American film had attempted for that era. It is the most expensive thing in the picture and it looks like a documentary.

He also designed the Corleone compound, the Lake Tahoe estate — deliberately colder, larger, and emptier than the Long Island house, which is the whole *Part II* thesis in architecture — and the Havana sequences.

Elsewhere: *Bonnie and Clyde*, *Zabriskie Point*, *The Conversation*, *Apocalypse Now*, *One from the Heart*, *Tucker*, and a long later collaboration with Wim Wenders and Roman Polanski.

## The editors

Three films, seven editors, and three Academy nominations without a win.

**William Reynolds** and **Peter Zinner** — *The Godfather* (1972), nominated. Zinner is generally credited with the assembly of the [baptism sequence](../analysis/baptism-sequence.md), which is one of the most-studied pieces of editing in cinema. He later won for *The Deer Hunter*.

**Peter Zinner, Barry Malkin, Richard Marks** — *Part II*, nominated. The double-timeline structure is theirs to execute, and the film previewed badly three weeks out precisely because of it; they attempted a restructure and ran out of time. Coppola has said the final reels are less finely timed than he wanted.

**Barry Malkin, Lisa Fruchtman, Walter Murch** — *Part III*, nominated.

**Walter Murch** deserves separate mention. He did not cut the first two films but is central to the Coppola circle: sound montage and re-recording on *The Godfather* — including the subway roar in the restaurant scene, one of the great sound decisions in film — and picture and sound on *The Conversation* and *Apocalypse Now*, for which he effectively invented modern film sound design and won two Oscars in one night. His book *In the Blink of an Eye* is the standard text on film editing.

**Barry Malkin** cut all three *Godfather* films in some capacity and was Coppola's most consistent editorial collaborator across four decades.

## Costume

**Anna Hill Johnstone** — *The Godfather* (1972), nominated. A New York theatre and film veteran (*On the Waterfront*, *Dog Day Afternoon*) whose work here is so period-accurate that it disappears. The specific achievement is the Corleone men's tailoring: heavy, slightly ill-fitting, and unmistakably 1945 rather than 1971's idea of 1945.

**Theadora Van Runkle** — *Part II*, nominated. She had come off *Bonnie and Clyde* (1967), whose berets and midi-skirts had genuinely changed how women dressed. Her *Part II* work covers three decades in two timelines.

**Milena Canonero** — *Part III*. A four-time Oscar winner elsewhere (*Barry Lyndon*, *Chariots of Fire*, *Marie Antoinette*, *The Grand Budapest Hotel*).

## Fred Roos — Casting, then Producer

Casting director on the first film and a producer on *Part II* (sharing its Best Picture Oscar) and *Part III*.

Roos (1934–2024) is the reason the film is full of stage actors nobody had heard of. He pushed for [Pacino](al-pacino.md), found [John Cazale](john-cazale.md), and brought in [Diane Keaton](diane-keaton.md), [Talia Shire](talia-shire.md), Richard Castellano, and Abe Vigoda. His casting instincts are the trilogy's least-discussed structural advantage: a cast of unknowns means nobody arrives carrying a previous role.

He was a lifelong Coppola collaborator — *The Conversation*, *Apocalypse Now*, *The Outsiders*, *Rumble Fish*, and *Megalopolis*.

## Carmine Coppola — Music

The director's father (1910–1991). A flautist for the NBC Symphony under Toscanini, and a composer who spent most of his life not being one.

He wrote the wedding band music for the first film and appears on screen as a piano player. He shared the **Academy Award for Best Original Dramatic Score** for [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md) with [Nino Rota](nino-rota.md). After Rota's death in 1979 he scored [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md) alone, working from Rota's themes, and was nominated for its song "Promise Me You'll Remember."

He also composed the score for the 1981 restoration of Abel Gance's *Napoléon*, which his son presented — a genuinely significant piece of film preservation history.
