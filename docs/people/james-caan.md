## Before

A Queens kid — the son of a kosher butcher, a football player at Michigan State, and briefly a rodeo rider, which he kept up for decades and which explains a lot about how he moved.

He trained at the **Neighborhood Playhouse** under Sanford Meisner, where his classmates included [Robert Duvall](robert-duvall.md) — a friendship that lasted sixty years — and where the building also contained Gene Hackman and Dustin Hoffman at various points. He worked steadily through the 1960s in television and in Howard Hawks's *El Dorado* (1966), and then broke through on television in ***Brian's Song*** (1971), playing the dying Chicago Bear Brian Piccolo. It got an Emmy nomination and made a large number of American men cry in public, which was not then a common experience.

---

## Sonny

He was in the mix for **Michael** — he screen-tested for it — but both he and Coppola preferred Sonny, and Paramount's approval of [Pacino](al-pacino.md) was made contingent on Caan taking the part.

He improvised heavily and Coppola kept most of it. The business with the wedding photographer, much of the volatility, and a good deal of the physical comedy in the family scenes is his. He took the street beating of [Carlo Rizzi](../characters/carlo-rizzi.md) seriously enough to injure his co-star Gianni Russo — a cracked rib and a chipped elbow, by several accounts.

He was nominated for Best Supporting Actor alongside Pacino and Duvall. The three split the vote; Joel Grey won for *Cabaret*.

He returned for one scene in [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md) — the 1941 flashback — reportedly for a fee equal to what he was paid for the whole of the first film.

---

## The mob thing

During and after production, Caan spent time with actual organized crime figures, notably **Carmine Persico** of the Colombo family, out of what he always described as research and fascination. It attracted federal attention; in 1982 the FBI recorded conversations between Caan and the mobster Anthony Fiato regarding Joe Pesci.

Nothing came of any of it. He also once posted a character reference for a reputed mobster, and spent the rest of his life fielding the same question, which he answered with the same joke.

---

## After

A superb and consistently undervalued 1970s: *The Gambler* (1974) — the film he called his best, and a role built directly on his own compulsive gambling — *Rollerball* (1975), *Funny Lady* (1975), *A Bridge Too Far* (1977), and Michael Mann's ***Thief*** (1981), a lean, blue-lit crime film that flopped and is now regarded as one of the best American films of its decade.

Then a five-year disappearance. Caan stopped working entirely after his sister's death from leukemia, and has described the period frankly as depression and cocaine. Coppola brought him back in *Gardens of Stone* (1987).

The third act was character work, and he was very good at it: ***Misery*** (1990), *Honeymoon in Vegas* (1992), *Bottle Rocket* (1996), *The Yards* (2000), ***Elf*** (2003) — which introduced him to an entire generation as Buddy's exasperated father — and the television series *Las Vegas* (2003–07).

He died on **July 6, 2022**, four months after the film's fiftieth anniversary, at 82. Pacino, De Niro, Duvall, and Streisand all issued tributes.
