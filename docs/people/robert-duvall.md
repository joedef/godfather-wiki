## Before

The son of a navy officer, raised largely in Annapolis. After two years in the Army he went to New York on the GI Bill and trained at the **Neighborhood Playhouse** under Sanford Meisner, alongside [James Caan](james-caan.md). He roomed with **Dustin Hoffman** in 1955 and later with **Gene Hackman** — three men in cheap apartments, all working as clerks and drivers, who between them would take a substantial share of the next thirty years of American acting.

Stage first, and seriously: he considered his Eddie Carbone in Arthur Miller's *A View from the Bridge* the turning point of his career.

His film debut was **Boo Radley** in *To Kill a Mockingbird* (1962) — a part with no dialogue, cast on Horton Foote's recommendation, in which a young actor stands in a doorway and communicates an entire life by being frightened of the light. *Bullitt*, *True Grit*, and *M\*A\*S\*H* followed.

---

## Tom Hagen

Coppola had worked with him on ***The Rain People*** (1969) and wanted him for Hagen from the very start. He is the only major casting decision on the film that was never contested by anybody.

The performance is a masterclass in playing competence. Hagen is the least demonstrative character in the trilogy and Duvall gives him an enormous amount of interior weather anyway — most visibly in the Hollywood sequence, where he absorbs Jack Woltz's abuse without a flicker, and in the moment Michael removes him as consigliere, where a lifetime's suspicion lands in about half a second of stillness.

He was nominated for Best Supporting Actor for the first film, in the same category as Caan and [Pacino](al-pacino.md).

He also appeared in Coppola's ***The Conversation*** (1974) and then, unforgettably, as **Lt. Col. Kilgore** in ***Apocalypse Now*** (1979) — *I love the smell of napalm in the morning* — which won him a BAFTA and a Golden Globe.

---

## Part III

He did not return, and the reason was money.

His account never varied across three decades: he objected not to Pacino being paid more, but to being paid a fraction of it. *If they paid Pacino twice what they paid me, that's fine, but not three or four times, which is what they did.*

Paramount would not move. The script wrote Hagen out — dead before the film opens, unexplained — and invented the lawyer B. J. Harrison for George Hamilton to fill the space.

It is the most consequential absence in the trilogy. See [Part III § The Duvall problem](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md#the-duvall-problem) and his own account [on Conan](../media/interviews.md#caan-duvall-and-the-reunions).

## The career

Seven decades, and startlingly few bad performances.

***Tender Mercies*** (1983) won him Best Actor — a washed-up country singer, and he insisted on writing and performing his own songs. ***The Great Santini*** (1979), ***The Apostle*** (1997, which he wrote, directed, and self-financed), *Godfather*, *Network*, *THX 1138*, *Colors*, *A Civil Action*, *Open Range*, *Get Low*, and *The Judge* (2014) all drew nominations or should have.

On television he won Emmys for ***Lonesome Dove*** (1989) — Gus McCrae was his own favorite of everything he did — and *Broken Trail* (2006).

Off screen: a serious tango dancer with studios in Argentina and the US, a horseman, and a Virginia farmer. He married four times and had no children; his fourth wife, Luciana Pedraza, shares his January 5 birthday.

He died at his farm in Middleburg, Virginia, on **February 15, 2026**, aged 95.
