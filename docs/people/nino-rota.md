## Before

A genuine prodigy: an oratorio at eleven, a three-act opera at thirteen. He trained in Milan and Rome under Pizzetti and Casella, and — on **Toscanini's** encouragement — spent 1930–32 in the United States at the **Curtis Institute** in Philadelphia, studying under Fritz Reiner. He took a literature degree at Milan in 1937 and ran the Liceo Musicale in Bari from 1950 to 1978.

He wrote constantly. More than 150 film scores, averaging around three a year, with **thirteen in 1954 alone**, alongside operas, ballets, concertos, and chamber music that he considered his real work and almost nobody has heard.

---

## Fellini

The defining partnership of both their careers, from *Lo Sceicco Bianco* (1952) through *La Strada*, *Nights of Cabiria*, ***La Dolce Vita***, ***8½***, *Juliet of the Spirits*, *Amarcord*, and *Casanova*.

Fellini's own description: *the most precious collaborator I have ever had… between us, immediately, a complete, total harmony.*

Rota's Fellini music is where the style is clearest — circus brass, waltzes, small sad melodies that seem to arrive from another room. It is music that refuses to underline. Which is exactly why it works for the Corleones.

## The Godfather

He wrote it in Rome. **Robert Evans hated it** on first hearing, thought it too highbrow, and pushed to replace it. [Coppola](francis-ford-coppola.md) fought him and won, on the argument that the Italianness of the score was the whole point.

The score is essentially three ideas:

**The main theme** — a solo trumpet, unaccompanied, over almost nothing. One voice, no family. It is the loneliest melody in a film about a family.

**The "Love Theme"** (*Parla Più Piano*) — the mandolin waltz attached to Sicily and to [Apollonia](../characters/apollonia-vitelli.md).

**The waltz** — the wedding and family music, which reappears throughout as a memory of before.

What's remarkable is the restraint. There is **no music at all** during the restaurant murders. None during the [baptism](../analysis/baptism-sequence.md) — that's Bach on a church organ, diegetic. None over Sonny at the tollbooth. Rota scores the family; he refuses to score the crime. See [Music](../analysis/music.md).

## The disqualification

The Academy nominated the score, and then **withdrew the nomination** when it emerged that the Love Theme had been adapted from Rota's own score for the 1958 Italian comedy ***Fortunella***.

The rule barred previously-used material. The music was Rota's, reused by Rota, from an obscure film nobody in Hollywood had seen — but the rule was the rule, and the nomination was rescinded. It remains one of the more absurd episodes in Academy history.

Two years later he won **Best Original Dramatic Score** for [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md), sharing it with **Carmine Coppola**, who wrote the additional material.

The AFI later ranked the *Godfather* score **#5** among the greatest film scores of all time — for the film whose nomination was cancelled.

---

## After

He died in Rome on **April 10, 1979**, aged 67, of coronary thrombosis. He had scored *Death on the Nile* the previous year.

His death is why [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md)'s score is **Carmine Coppola**'s alone, working from Rota's themes — competent, faithful, and audibly not the same hand.
