## Before

Los Angeles-born, raised Free Methodist, and by her own account a person with no obvious plan. She understudied in the original Broadway production of ***Hair*** (1968) and declined to perform the optional nude scene — a detail she told for fifty years with visible enjoyment.

In 1969 she was cast opposite **Woody Allen** in his play *Play It Again, Sam*, earning a Tony nomination and beginning a personal and professional relationship that would define the next two decades of her career.

---

## Kay

She was 25, cast largely for her oddness — Coppola wanted someone who would look genuinely out of place in that house — and she had not read the novel.

The part is routinely dismissed as thin, and it isn't; it's *outside*, by design. Kay is the only sustained non-Corleone viewpoint in the trilogy, and every piece of exposition in the first act is delivered to her. What Keaton does with it is register, precisely and without italics, the exact moment a normal person understands what she has married into. See [Kay Adams](../characters/kay-adams.md).

Her best work is in [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md) — the abortion scene, which is the only successful act of resistance anybody mounts against Michael in two films, and which she plays without a shred of self-pity.

She has been unfailingly self-deprecating about the whole thing, describing the first shoot as bewildering and her own contribution as small. Almost nobody agrees.

---

## After

***Annie Hall*** (1977) is the one. The character was named for her — Diane Hall, called Annie — and built out of her mannerisms, her verbal tics, and her borrowed-menswear wardrobe, which promptly changed how a generation of women dressed. She won Best Actress.

Eight films with Allen between 1971 and 1993. Then a serious dramatic career of her own: **Looking for Mr. Goodbar** (1977), ***Reds*** (1981, nominated), *Shoot the Moon* (1982), *Crimes of the Heart*, *Marvin's Room* (1996, nominated), and ***Something's Gotta Give*** (2003, nominated) — a $125 million hit at 57, in a genre that had stopped writing parts for women her age.

She also directed — *Heaven* (1987), *Unstrung Heroes* (1995), and television — and had a substantial second identity as a photographer, a preservationist of Los Angeles architecture, and a serial restorer of houses.

She never married. She adopted two children in her fifties. Asked about it repeatedly for forty years, she generally cited her mother's sacrifices and declined to elaborate further.

She died on **October 11, 2025**, aged 79.
