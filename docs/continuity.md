# Continuity

There is no single *Godfather* canon. There is a novel, four cuts of three films, two chronological reassemblies, three continuation novels, a novel-that-isn't-quite-a-sequel, and a video game — and they contradict each other on matters as basic as who dies and when.

This page defines the tiers this wiki uses. Pages elsewhere label claims by tier when a claim is version-specific.

---

## The tiers

### Tier 1 — The films

The three theatrical features and *Coda*. Where they conflict with anything else, they win, because they are what the phrase "The Godfather" means to almost everyone who uses it.

| Version | Status |
| --- | --- |
| *The Godfather* (1972) | Primary |
| *The Godfather Part II* (1974) | Primary |
| *The Godfather Part III* (1990) | Primary |
| *The Godfather Coda: The Death of Michael Corleone* (2020) | Coppola's preferred version of Part III; supersedes it where they differ |

### Tier 2 — Puzo's novels

[*The Godfather*](books/the-godfather-1969.md) (1969) is the source, and it is authoritative on everything the films chose not to depict — Vito's Prohibition-era operations, Johnny Fontane's Hollywood career, the internal logic of the Families. It is *not* authoritative where the films revised it, which is often.

[*The Sicilian*](books/the-sicilian-1984.md) (1984) is officially a sequel — it fills in Michael's Sicilian exile between the two halves of the 1972 film — but it functions as a Salvatore Giuliano novel with Michael bolted to the frame. Its continuity is loose enough that the 1987 film adaptation removed Michael entirely for rights reasons.

### Tier 3 — The recuts

[*The Godfather Saga*](films/recuts-and-restorations.md) (1977), *The Godfather Epic* (1981), and *The Godfather Trilogy: 1901–1980* (1992) reorder existing material chronologically and restore [deleted scenes](films/deleted-scenes.md). The restored scenes are genuinely useful continuity — they answer questions the theatrical cuts leave open, like what happened to Michael in Sicily after the bombing, and how Vito's Prohibition business actually ran. But Coppola has treated them as supplementary, not corrective.

### Tier 4 — The continuation novels

[*The Godfather Returns*](books/the-godfather-returns-2004.md) (2004) and [*The Godfather's Revenge*](books/the-godfathers-revenge-2006.md) (2006) by Mark Winegardner, and [*The Family Corleone*](books/the-family-corleone-2012.md) (2012) by Ed Falco. All three are estate-authorized. All three are also, from the films' point of view, apocrypha: they invent major characters (Nick Geraci) who cannot exist in the films because the films account for those years differently.

### Tier 5 — Everything else

The [video games](reference/video-games.md), *[The Offer](films/the-offer-2022.md)* (which is about the making of the film, not the fiction), parodies, and homages. Interesting; not continuity.

## Known contradictions

### How Vito's consigliere succession works

- **Novel:** Genco Abbandando is consigliere and dies of cancer on the day of Connie's wedding; Tom Hagen has been acting consigliere for some time.
- **1972 film:** The Genco deathbed scene was shot and cut. In the theatrical film, Hagen is simply consigliere, and Genco is a name on the olive oil company.
- **Restored cuts:** The hospital scene is reinstated, restoring the novel's version.

### When Fredo went to Las Vegas, and what he did there

The novel puts Fredo in Las Vegas learning the casino business under Moe Greene, badly. *Part II* implies a longer and more humiliating apprenticeship. Winegardner rewrites the period substantially, giving Fredo a secret life the films do not support and arguably contradict.

### Michael's Sicilian exile

- **1972 film:** A montage, a marriage, a car bomb. Roughly two years, mostly elided.
- **The Sicilian:** The same two years, reframed so that Michael spends them entangled in the Giuliano affair — an event the film gives no hint of.

These are not reconcilable so much as parallel. Read *The Sicilian* as a novel that borrows Michael, not as a missing chapter.

### Tom Hagen's death

- **Part III:** Hagen is dead before the film opens; Michael refers to it obliquely. This exists because [Robert Duvall](people/robert-duvall.md) and Paramount could not agree on money. In-universe it is unexplained.
- **The Godfather's Revenge:** Winegardner supplies a death for Hagen that the films neither confirm nor deny.

### Vincent's legitimacy and succession

*Part III* has Michael formally hand the family to [Vincent Mancini](characters/vincent-mancini.md), Sonny's illegitimate son, who takes the Corleone name. No novel written before 1990 knows Vincent exists. Winegardner, writing after, works around him.

### The unmade Part IV

The proposed fourth film would have run 1930s Vito (De Niro) and young Sonny against 1980s Vincent. It was never made; Puzo's material for it became [*The Family Corleone*](books/the-family-corleone-2012.md). See [The Unmade Part IV](films/unmade-part-iv.md).

## Reading the tier labels

Where a claim on this wiki is true in one version only, it's marked inline:

> Genco Abbandando dies on the morning of Connie's wedding. *(novel; restored cuts — not in the 1972 theatrical film)*

Where versions agree, no label appears. Where they conflict irreconcilably, the page says so rather than picking a winner.
