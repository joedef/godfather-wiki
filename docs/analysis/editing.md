# Editing

Three films, seven editors, three Academy nominations, no wins. The trilogy's editing is famous for one sequence and interesting for the reason that sequence works — which is that everything around it is so patient.

---

## The paradox

*The Godfather* is 175 minutes long and moves faster than most 95-minute films. *Part II* is 202 minutes and holds an audience through two timelines in two languages.

The mechanism is **contrast, not pace**. The trilogy's default is long takes, locked frames, and scenes allowed to run past the point where a conventional edit would cut. Because that's the baseline, any acceleration registers as violence — and because the films are so long, the slow scenes have room to do character work that a tighter film would have to state in dialogue.

The corollary: **almost nothing in these films is cut for coverage.** Willis and Coppola shot fewer angles than a studio picture of the period normally would, which limited the editors' options and forced decisions on set. That's a risk. It also produces a film with no fat in it, because there was never any spare footage to be tempted by.

## The restaurant hold

The best editing decision in the first film is a refusal to cut.

Michael returns from the toilet with the gun and sits down. Sollozzo talks. And the film **holds on Pacino's face** while a subway grinds past outside, deafening, for far longer than is comfortable.

The scene has every ingredient for a fast cutting pattern — a hidden weapon, two targets, a countdown — and does the opposite. What the audience experiences during that hold is Michael's own experience: too much time, nothing to do with it, and a decision that is already made and still hasn't happened.

Then the actual killing takes about four seconds.

## The tollbooth

The trilogy's one genuinely fast sequence, and the exception that proves the method.

[Sonny](../characters/sonny-corleone.md) at the causeway is cut in the grammar of an action film: rapid, fragmentary, many angles, escalating. It is the most heavily covered sequence in the picture — three days of shooting, a purpose-built tollbooth, a car pre-drilled for squibs — and it is the only time the first film uses that vocabulary.

It works because it's alone. Ninety minutes of patience, and then thirty seconds of chaos, and the chaos means something because the film has never done it before.

## The [baptism](baptism-sequence.md)

The famous one, generally credited to **Peter Zinner**, working with **William Reynolds**.

Its reputation is as a montage. It's better understood as a **sound edit**: the church organ runs continuous and unbroken across five locations, and the picture is cut against a musical bed that never acknowledges the cuts. The images are discontinuous; the audio insists they are one event.

The escalation is textbook — shot durations shorten, the organ registration builds, and the priest's questions land on structural beats — but the invention is the unbroken sound.

Its influence has been enormous and mostly harmful. *Scarface*, *The Untouchables*, *The Departed*, and a thousand television imitators have run the ceremony-intercut-with-violence construction, and nearly all of them **score the violence**, which converts a theological claim into an ironic one.

## The double timeline

[*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md)'s structure was executed by **Peter Zinner, Barry Malkin, and Richard Marks**, and it nearly ended the film.

The transitions carry the argument: each cut between Vito's rise and Michael's decay is a comparison the film never states. See [Structure & Parallel Editing](structure-and-parallel-editing.md).

Three weeks before release it previewed badly — the crosscutting reported as exhausting and confusing. The editors attempted a restructure and ran out of time. Coppola has said the final reels are less finely timed than he wanted, and if you look for it, the last forty minutes do move differently from the first hundred and fifty.

The dissolves are worth noticing. *Part II* transitions between eras with slow dissolves rather than hard cuts, and often across a matched compositional element — which is how a film cutting between 1917 and 1958 every fifteen minutes never once disorients you.

## Walter Murch

He didn't cut the first two films, but he is central to why they sound like they do.

Murch was **sound montage and re-recording** on *The Godfather*, and the **subway roar in the restaurant** is his. He then did picture and sound on *The Conversation* and *Apocalypse Now* — effectively inventing modern film sound design in the process and winning two Oscars in one night — and finally cut [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md) with Barry Malkin and Lisa Fruchtman.

His book ***In the Blink of an Eye*** is the standard text on film editing, and its central claim — that the correct cut point is the one that matches the audience's blink, which is to say their moment of accepting a completed thought — is the best available description of how the *Godfather* films are assembled.

## Coda, and what a re-edit can do

The most instructive editing lesson in the franchise is [*The Godfather Coda*](../films/the-godfather-coda-2020.md) (2020).

Same footage. Four minutes shorter. Two structural moves: the Vatican Bank meeting relocated from the middle to the opening, and Michael's death removed from the ending.

**Rotten Tomatoes: 67% → 86%. Metacritic: 60 → 76.**

The sample is smaller and thirty years of rehabilitation had already happened, so the numbers overstate it. But the direction is real, and the cause is identifiable: reordering the first ten minutes changes what the audience thinks the film is about, and therefore what every subsequent scene means.

That's the whole argument for editing as authorship, demonstrated on a controlled experiment nobody planned to run.
