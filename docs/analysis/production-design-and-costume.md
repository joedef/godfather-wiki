# Production Design & Costume

[Dean Tavoularis](../people/the-crew.md) designed all three films and won an Academy Award for the second. His work is the trilogy's least-discussed authorship, because good production design is built not to be noticed.

---

## The two houses

The clearest single argument the design makes, and it's made entirely without dialogue.

**The Long Beach compound** (1945–1955) is a walled cluster of dark brick houses on Long Island. Interiors are brown, crowded, and low-ceilinged: heavy furniture, patterned wallpaper, framed photographs on every surface, food constantly present. The kitchen is a main room. People are always in shot behind other people. It is a house that a dozen people live in and it feels like it.

**The Lake Tahoe estate** ([*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md), 1958) is vast, pale, and modern. High ceilings, big windows, northern light off water, and enormous empty rooms with nobody in them. Michael's office is a boathouse-scaled space where a man sits alone at a desk. The kitchen is somewhere else, staffed.

Michael has moved his family from a house to a compound to an estate, and each move has removed people from the frame. By the last shot of *Part II* — a man in a garden chair by a lake — the design has been telling you for two hours what the scene finally shows.

## 1917 Little Italy

Tavoularis's masterpiece, and the most expensive thing in *Part II*.

Several blocks of period New York, built and dressed on a stretch of **East 6th Street in Manhattan**: pushcarts, laundry lines, hand-painted shopfronts, fire escapes, the *festa* with its arch of lights, and a density of extras and incidental business that no American film had attempted for that period.

The reason it matters is that it isn't nostalgic. It's crowded, dirty, loud, and *poor*. The Fanucci sequence works because the neighborhood is legible as a place where a man with a knife and a white suit could plausibly tax everyone, and where a young man who quietly removes him would be regarded as a benefactor.

It won Tavoularis, **Angelo Graham**, and set decorator **George R. Nelson** the Academy Award for Best Art Direction.

## Vito's study

The single most important set in the trilogy, and essentially one room with shutters.

It is dark at all times, regardless of the hour outside. The blinds are closed. There is a large desk, a leather chair, and a door that opens onto a corridor where people wait. The window overlooks the sunlit wedding, and the film's opening shot spends five minutes pulling back to reveal that relationship: **darkness in here, daylight out there, and a door between them.**

Everything that matters in the first film happens in this room or is decided in it. And Michael's version of it — the Tahoe office, the Nevada desk — is the same room built to a scale that says the man in it no longer has to sit near anyone.

See [Doors, Thresholds & Frames](doors-and-thresholds.md).

## Costume

Three designers, three nominations, no wins.

**Anna Hill Johnstone** — *The Godfather* (1972), nominated. A New York theatre and film veteran (*On the Waterfront*, *Dog Day Afternoon*). Her achievement is invisibility: the tailoring is heavy, slightly loose, and unmistakably **1945 rather than 1971's idea of 1945** — wide lapels, high-waisted trousers, hats that are worn rather than styled.

**Theadora Van Runkle** — *Part II*, nominated. She came from *Bonnie and Clyde* (1967), whose berets and midi skirts genuinely changed how women dressed. *Part II* asks her to cover three decades across two timelines and she does it without ever letting the clothes become the subject.

**Milena Canonero** — *Part III*. Four Oscars elsewhere (*Barry Lyndon*, *Chariots of Fire*, *Marie Antoinette*, *The Grand Budapest Hotel*).

---

## What the tailoring tells you

The costume design is a status readout, and it's consistent enough to track:

**Vito** wears the same dark, well-made, unfashionable suit for thirty years. It fits. It is not expensive-looking. He has nothing to prove and dresses like a man who owns a business.

**Sonny** wears louder colors, sharper cuts, and looser collars. He looks like a man who wants to be looked at, which is why he dies.

**Michael's** progression is the arc in fabric. He arrives in a **Marine dress uniform** — the only person at the wedding not in a suit, and the only person wearing something a government issued him. In the middle films he is in dark, correct, expensive tailoring that is slightly too big through the shoulders, because Pacino is small and the film wants the clothes to be wearing him a little. By [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md) he is dressed like a European industrialist.

**Fredo** is the tell. He dresses better than his position — brighter, flashier, more Vegas — because he is trying to look like somebody.

**Kay** is the outsider in fabric: New England wool, plain colors, no jewelry, at a wedding where every other woman is in silk and gold.

**Joey Zasa** in *Part III* wears the best clothes in the film, which is exactly why Michael despises him.

## The props that do work

**Oranges.** They appear before nearly every death or attempted death. See [Doors, Thresholds & Frames § The oranges](doors-and-thresholds.md#the-oranges).

**Food.** Constant, and always a transaction. Clemenza's sauce lesson is an induction. The cannoli is a wife's order carried out of a murder scene. Altobello dies of cannoli in [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md). Nobody in these films eats without it meaning something.

**The Genco Pura sign.** The olive oil company's name on the office wall is the only visible trace of the [dead consigliere](../characters/minor-characters.md) whose deathbed scene was cut from the theatrical film.

**Photographs.** The Corleone houses are full of framed family pictures, and the film returns to them at funerals. The last thing the trilogy does with this motif is the 1941 flashback — a family photograph staged as a scene, with one person left at the table.
