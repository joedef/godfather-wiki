# Legacy & Influence

What the trilogy did to films, to television, to the American language, and — genuinely — to organized crime.

---

## To the film industry

**It changed what a hit could be.** Before March 1972, a serious, slow, three-hour, R-rated, subtitled-in-places period drama with a difficult ending was not a blockbuster. *The Godfather* was the highest-grossing film ever made, and it was those things.

The immediate consequence was the **New Hollywood** period's peak: for roughly five years, American studios funded difficult films by young directors because the most profitable film in history had been one. *The Conversation*, *Chinatown*, *Nashville*, *Taxi Driver*, *Apocalypse Now*, and *The Deer Hunter* all exist in the space *The Godfather* opened.

The other consequence was the opposite one. *The Godfather* also invented the wide-release-plus-saturation-marketing pattern that *Jaws* (1975) and *Star Wars* (1977) perfected — and which ended New Hollywood.

**It invented the numbered sequel.** [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md) was the first major American film to use the convention, over Paramount's objection. Every *Part II* since exists downstream of that argument.

**It made the prestige sequel possible.** Before 1974, a sequel was a cash-in by definition. After *Part II* won six Academy Awards including Best Picture, it wasn't.

---

## To the gangster film

The genre had existed since *Little Caesar* (1931) and *Scarface* (1932), and the pre-1972 model was consistent: crime is a fever, the gangster rises fast, and the film ends with him dead in the street as a warning.

*The Godfather* replaced it with something else entirely: **crime as a family business with a succession problem**. The gangster is no longer an outsider who breaks in; he is a manager with a portfolio. Nobody dies in the street as a moral lesson. The ones who die are inconvenient.

Almost every crime film since has had to position itself relative to that:

- ***Goodfellas*** (1990) is the explicit rebuttal — Scorsese's argument that the life is squalid, funny, and stupid rather than operatic, and that the Corleones' dignity is the lie.
- ***Once Upon a Time in America*** (1984) — Leone finally making the film he passed on *The Godfather* to make.
- ***Carlito's Way***, ***Donnie Brasco***, ***A Bronx Tale***, ***Road to Perdition***, ***Gomorrah***, ***The Irishman***.
- ***Scarface*** (1983) — Pacino again, doing the opposite: all appetite, no restraint.

## To television

**The Sopranos** (1999–2007) is the largest debt in television, and it is paid explicitly. David Chase's characters *quote* the films constantly, argue about them, and measure themselves against them — Silvio does the Michael impression, and the show's whole comic engine is men who learned how to be gangsters from a movie.

But the deeper inheritance is [*Part II*'s structure](../analysis/structure-and-parallel-editing.md): the dual timeline, the past commenting on the present, the refusal to explain, the willingness to let a protagonist become unlikeable. That grammar runs through *The Sopranos* into *Mad Men*, *Breaking Bad*, *Boardwalk Empire*, *Peaky Blinders*, and *Better Call Saul*.

**Personnel, too.** [Dominic Chianese](../characters/minor-characters.md) (Johnny Ola) became Uncle Junior. **Tony Sirico**, uncredited as a Rosato hood in *Part II*, became Paulie Walnuts.

## To the language

Phrases that entered general English from a 1969 novel and a 1972 film:

- **"An offer he can't refuse"** — AFI's #2 movie quote of all time
- **"Going to the mattresses"**
- **"Sleeps with the fishes"**
- **"Leave the gun, take the cannoli"** — improvised on set by Richard Castellano
- **"Consigliere"** — the office is real; its use in English business slang is the film's doing
- **"Godfather"** as a term for any dominant figure in a field
- **"Just when I thought I was out, they pull me back in"**

See [Quotes](../data/quotes.md) and [Glossary § What Puzo invented](glossary.md#what-puzo-invented).

## To organized crime

This is the strangest part of the legacy and it's documented.

[Puzo had never met a gangster](../people/mario-puzo.md). He built the Mafia in the novel from newspaper files and the Kefauver and Valachi hearing transcripts. And then **the real organization began performing the fiction**.

FBI surveillance recordings from the 1970s onward capture members using Puzo's phrasing, referencing the films, and in some cases explicitly citing them as models of how a man of respect should conduct himself. Prosecutors and agents have described a measurable shift in how mobsters presented themselves after 1972 — more formality, more talk of honor and family, a self-image borrowed wholesale from a film.

The cleanest illustration remains [Joe Colombo](real-world-mafia.md#joe-colombo-and-the-league), who ran a crime family and simultaneously ran the anti-defamation campaign against the film depicting one.

The town of **Corleone**, which Puzo named in 1969, went on to produce the faction that dominated Cosa Nostra through the 1980s and murdered the magistrates Falcone and Borsellino. It now has an anti-Mafia museum and a permanent public-relations problem it did not choose.

## To food, business, and everything else

**The cannoli.** One improvised line has kept a pastry in the American vocabulary for fifty years.

**Business writing.** *The Godfather* is one of the most-cited films in management literature — succession planning, delegation, negotiation, and the phrase "an offer he can't refuse" appear in seminars, and there have been multiple books explicitly framing Vito Corleone as a leadership model. This is either evidence for Coppola's thesis about capitalism or the single most complete failure to understand a film in modern history, and possibly both.

**The Fujita cover.** S. Neil Fujita's marionette-strings design went from a book jacket to the film's poster to a permanent piece of visual shorthand.

**Parody.** *The Simpsons* has returned to it dozens of times. *You've Got Mail* has a whole running argument about it. *The Freshman* (1990) cast Brando as a Vito parody. *Analyze This* (1999) is a *Godfather* joke with Robert De Niro in it. *The Godfather* is one of a very small number of films that a general audience can be assumed to have seen, which is what makes it parody-able at all.

## The standing

| | |
| --- | --- |
| **AFI's 100 Years…100 Movies** | #3 (1998), **#2 (2007)** |
| **AFI 10 Top 10 — Gangster** | **#1** |
| **National Film Registry** | 1990 (first year of eligibility) and 1993 |
| **Sight & Sound directors' poll** | *Part II* at **#2** in 2002 |
| **Writers Guild of America** | *The Godfather* **#2** greatest screenplay ever |
| **Metacritic** | **100/100** — one of very few |

Its reputation has been stable for fifty years in a way almost no other film's is. That is itself remarkable: the canon churns constantly, and *The Godfather* has been at or near the top of it since the decade it was released.
