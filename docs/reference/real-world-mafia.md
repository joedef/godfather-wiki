# Real-World Mafia

What's documented, what's inferred, and what Puzo made up.

!!! warning "Read this first"
    Organized crime history is full of unverifiable claims, and *Godfather* history is worse, because fifty years of retelling have blurred the fiction into the record. Entries below are marked **documented**, **inferred**, or **disputed**. Where an account rests on a single interested source, the page says so.

---

## What Puzo invented

[Mario Puzo](../people/mario-puzo.md) had **never met a gangster** when he wrote the novel. This is documented, repeatedly stated by him, and the most surprising fact about the whole enterprise. He built the Mafia in *The Godfather* out of newspaper morgues and the published transcripts of the **Kefauver Committee** (1950–51) and the **Valachi hearings** (1963).

What's real in the novel is the *family* — the food, the fatalism, the way respect functions as currency. That came from his own Neapolitan-immigrant childhood in Hell's Kitchen and from the novel he'd already written about it, [*The Fortunate Pilgrim*](../people/mario-puzo.md).

The criminal architecture is largely invented, and it was invented persuasively enough that **the real thing adopted it**:

- *"Make him an offer he can't refuse"* is Puzo's phrase and has since been used by actual criminals.
- The public understanding of *consigliere*, of the Commission, and of the godfather-as-boss is downstream of a 1969 novel.
- FBI surveillance from the 1970s onward records members using Puzo's phrasing and, in some cases, citing the film as a model of comportment.

The most-cited example: **Joe Colombo's** own Italian-American Civil Rights League campaigned against the film for portraying a Mafia its founder simultaneously ran. There is no cleaner illustration of the feedback loop.

## The Commission

**Documented.** Established by **Charles "Lucky" Luciano** in 1931 after the Castellammarese War, as a governing body to arbitrate between families and prevent the open warfare that had just killed Joe Masseria and Salvatore Maranzano. Five New York families plus Chicago and Buffalo.

The **real Five Families**: Bonanno, Colombo (formerly Profaci), Gambino (formerly Mangano/Anastasia), Genovese (formerly Luciano), and Lucchese.

Vito's peace conference in [*The Godfather*](../films/the-godfather-1972.md) is a version of a Commission sitting, staged as a board meeting, which is what contemporaneous accounts describe.

The **Apalachin meeting** of November 1957 — a Commission summit in upstate New York broken up by a state trooper, sending dozens of bosses fleeing through the woods in expensive suits — is the event that finally forced J. Edgar Hoover to concede that a national organization existed. *Part II*'s Senate committee sequence sits in its aftermath.

## Vito Corleone

**Composite; inferred.** No single model. The most-cited components:

**Frank Costello** (1891–1973) — the "Prime Minister of the Underworld," who ran on political influence rather than violence and cultivated judges and politicians exactly as Vito does. His **1951 Kefauver testimony** was televised; he refused to let his face be shown, producing the famous footage of only his hands, and his voice was a distinctive rasp — which is what [Brando](../people/marlon-brando.md) listened to and copied.

**Carlo Gambino** (1902–1976) — quiet, courteous, patient, and the most successful boss of his era, who died in bed.

**Vito Genovese** (1897–1969) — the first name, and the narcotics conflict. Genovese pushed into heroin against the judgment of others and was ultimately convicted on a narcotics charge.

**Joseph Bonanno** (1905–2002) — the family-as-tradition self-presentation, and a memoir (*A Man of Honor*) that reads as though he had seen the film.

## Meyer Lansky and Hyman Roth

**Direct, and acknowledged.** [Hyman Roth](../characters/hyman-roth.md) is an unusually close portrait of **Meyer Lansky** (1902–1983).

Documented parallels: the Syndicate's financial architect rather than a street boss; the Havana casino operation under **Batista**, and its total loss to the Cuban Revolution in a week; the modest Miami Beach house and the businessman's self-presentation; the years spent seeking a country that would take him — Lansky was **refused entry to Israel** under the Law of Return in 1972 after an extended legal fight and was returned to the US.

Roth's line — *Michael, we're bigger than U.S. Steel* — is adapted from a remark attributed to Lansky.

Lansky reportedly saw *Part II* and objected to the accent. He died of lung cancer in Miami Beach in 1983, having never been convicted of anything significant.

**The Havana sequence** is [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md)'s most historically specific stretch: the American investors, the Batista apparatus, the cake iced as a map of Cuba divided among corporate men, and the whole thing ending at midnight on December 31, 1958, which is when Batista actually fled.

## Bugsy Siegel and Moe Greene

**Direct.** **Benjamin "Bugsy" Siegel** (1906–1947): Lansky's partner from childhood, who took Syndicate money to Las Vegas and built the **Flamingo**, ran it catastrophically over budget, and was shot dead in a Beverly Hills living room in June 1947 — one round taking out his eye.

[Moe Greene](../characters/moe-greene.md) is shot through the eye on a massage table. Puzo and Coppola pair him with Roth exactly as the history pairs Siegel with Lansky, and Roth's *we were like brothers* speech is the film acknowledging it.

## The Valachi hearings

**Direct.** **Joseph Valachi** (1904–1971), a Genovese soldier, testified before the **McClellan Committee** in **October 1963** — the first made member ever to break omertà publicly, on live television. He gave the American public the phrase ***Cosa Nostra***, described the structure and the initiation, and named names.

[*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md)'s Senate hearing sequence stages it directly: the room, the photographers, the senators, the counsel. [Frank Pentangeli](../characters/frank-pentangeli.md) is the Valachi figure, and Coppola has confirmed the parallel.

Valachi died in prison in 1971. Peter Maas's book about him, *The Valachi Papers*, was published in 1968 — a year before *The Godfather* — and was one of Puzo's sources.

## Joe Colombo and the League

**Documented, and genuinely strange.**

**Joseph Colombo** (1923–1978) was the boss of the Colombo family and, simultaneously, the founder in 1970 of the **Italian-American Civil Rights League** — an anti-defamation organization that was genuinely popular, drew tens of thousands to rallies at Columbus Circle, and successfully pressured the Justice Department to stop using the word "Mafia" in press releases.

The League organized against *The Godfather*. [Albert S. Ruddy](../people/albert-s-ruddy.md)'s car windows were shot out. Permits evaporated.

Ruddy met Colombo directly and reached an agreement in **February 1971**: the words *Mafia* and *Cosa Nostra* struck from the script, premiere proceeds to the League's hospital fund. Coppola has always maintained the words appeared twice and cost nothing.

**On June 28, 1971** — during production — Colombo was shot in the head at a League rally at Columbus Circle by Jerome Johnson, who was himself immediately killed. Colombo survived, paralyzed and effectively without speech, and died in 1978. The killing is generally attributed to **Joey Gallo**, who was himself murdered at Umberto's Clam House in April 1972, three weeks after the film opened.

**Lenny Montana**, who plays [Luca Brasi](../characters/luca-brasi.md), was a Colombo family associate. That much is documented. Broader claims about mob presence on set are **disputed** and rest largely on interested accounts — see [Reference § standing warning](index.md#a-standing-warning-about-this-material).

## The Sinatra rumor

**Disputed, and probably wrong in its strong form.**

The claim: [Johnny Fontane](../characters/johnny-fontane.md) is **Frank Sinatra**, and the horse's head dramatizes mob intervention that won Sinatra the role in *From Here to Eternity* (1953).

**Documented:** Sinatra's organized crime associations, including with Sam Giancana, were real and extensively investigated. Sinatra was furious about the novel and confronted Puzo publicly in a restaurant. The career-revival-through-a-war-picture parallel is genuinely striking.

**Not documented:** that Sinatra got the *Eternity* role through mob pressure. This has never been substantiated; the credible accounts credit his own campaigning, a very low salary, and Harry Cohn's calculation. Puzo denied basing Fontane on him.

The honest summary: Fontane is a composite of a recognizable mid-century type, Sinatra is the most famous instance of it, Sinatra believed it was about him, and that is not the same thing.

Sinatra later agreed to play **Don Altobello** in [*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md) and couldn't clear the schedule.

## The Vatican Bank affair

**Documented, and *Part III* is closer to the record than anything in the first two films.**

**Archbishop Paul Marcinkus** (1922–2006), the Chicago-born head of the Istituto per le Opere di Religione (the Vatican Bank), presided over its entanglement with **Banco Ambrosiano**, which collapsed in 1982 with over a billion dollars missing. Italian authorities issued a warrant; the Vatican asserted diplomatic immunity and he was never tried. → **Archbishop Gilday**.

**Roberto Calvi** (1920–1982), chairman of Banco Ambrosiano, known as "God's Banker," was found **hanging beneath Blackfriars Bridge in London** in June 1982 with bricks in his pockets and cash in his clothes. Ruled suicide, then an open verdict, then formally reclassified as murder in 2002. Five people were tried and acquitted in 2007. It has never been solved. → **Frederick Keinszig** — whose name comes from Manuela Kleinszig, an associate of a figure later indicted in the case.

**Michele Sindona** (1920–1986), a Sicilian financier with Vatican and Mafia ties, was convicted of ordering a murder and died in prison after drinking coffee laced with cyanide, two days into a life sentence.

**Giulio Andreotti** (1919–2013), seven times Italian prime minister, was tried in the 1990s for Mafia association. The verdicts are contested and complicated: acquitted on some counts, with an appeals finding that he had maintained associations up to 1980 which were then time-barred. → **Don Licio Lucchesi**, right down to the heavy spectacles. The line Calò delivers before killing him — *power wears out those who don't have it* — is Andreotti's own.

**Pope John Paul I** (Albino Luciani) reigned **33 days** in 1978 and died suddenly. The Vatican's handling of the announcement was chaotic enough to fuel a permanent conspiracy literature, most prominently David Yallop's *In God's Name* (1984). The medical consensus is a heart attack. → **Cardinal Lamberto**.

## Salvatore Giuliano

**Documented; the basis of [*The Sicilian*](../books/the-sicilian-1984.md).**

**Salvatore Giuliano** (1922–1950) killed a *carabiniere* at twenty while smuggling grain and became a bandit in the mountains around Montelepre. He redistributed seized grain and land, commanded genuine popular loyalty, and was courted and used by the Sicilian separatist movement, the landowners, the Christian Democrats, and the Mafia.

He was implicated in the **Portella della Ginestra massacre** of May 1, 1947 — eleven May Day celebrants shot dead — whose authorship has never been settled.

He was found dead in a courtyard in Castelvetrano in July 1950. The official account, a shootout with police, was disbelieved immediately. His cousin **Gaspare Pisciotta**, who confessed to killing him, was poisoned in prison in 1954 before he could testify further.

Francesco Rosi's ***Salvatore Giuliano*** (1962) is the definitive film on the subject and is far better than [Cimino's 1987 adaptation](../films/the-sicilian-1987.md).

## The town of Corleone

**Documented, and an accident of timing.**

Corleone is a real town of about 11,000 in the province of Palermo. Puzo picked the name in the 1960s.

It then produced a genuinely disproportionate share of Cosa Nostra's leadership. The ***Corleonesi*** faction — **Luciano Leggio**, then **Totò Riina** and **Bernardo Provenzano** — took control of the Sicilian Mafia through the 1970s and 80s in a campaign of extraordinary violence, and ran the bombings that killed the anti-Mafia magistrates **Giovanni Falcone** and **Paolo Borsellino** in 1992.

Riina was captured in 1993 and died in prison in 2017; Provenzano was captured in 2006 after forty-three years as a fugitive and died in 2016.

The town today has an anti-Mafia museum, the **CIDMA**, and a complicated relationship with a novel that made its name a metonym for the thing it has spent thirty years trying to escape.
