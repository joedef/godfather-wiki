# Locations

Roughly **90% of *The Godfather* was shot in and around New York City** — a reported 120+ distinct locations — with the remainder in California and Sicily. This page covers the significant ones and what's still there.

---

## New York

### The Corleone compound

**110 Longfellow Avenue, Todt Hill, Staten Island.**

A real house on a real street. The production dressed the neighboring properties and built the compound wall out of **styrofoam**. The wedding scene used 750+ local extras.

It is a private residence and has been sold several times since, most recently for figures that reflect what it is. **Do not go and knock on the door.**

### Sonny's tollbooth

**Mitchel Field, Uniondale, Long Island** — the former airfield, not an actual toll plaza.

Three tollbooths, guard rails, and billboards were built for the sequence, shot over three days beginning **June 22, 1971**, at a cost of over $100,000. A 1941 Lincoln Continental was pre-drilled with hundreds of holes for squibs.

The site is now largely developed; nothing of the set remains. See [Sonny Corleone](../characters/sonny-corleone.md).

### Louis' Restaurant

The Sollozzo/McCluskey murder was shot at **Old Luna Restaurant, 2373 Arthur Avenue, in the Belmont section of the Bronx** — the neighborhood that is still, genuinely, New York's best Italian food district.

The building has changed hands and function since. Arthur Avenue itself is very much alive.

### The Genco Pura offices / Little Italy

**Mott Street** and the surrounding blocks in Manhattan's Little Italy carried the olive oil business and much of the street work.

For [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md), [Dean Tavoularis](../people/the-crew.md) built a far more ambitious version: several blocks of **1917 Little Italy on East 6th Street**, dressed to a density no American film had attempted for that period. It won the Academy Award for Art Direction.

### Barzini's death

**The New York Supreme Court building, 60 Centre Street, Foley Square.**

Al Neri, in a stolen police uniform, shoots [Barzini](../characters/barzini-and-the-five-families.md) on the steps in daylight. The building is still there, still a courthouse, and instantly recognizable — it has appeared in a great many films since, most persistently in the *Law & Order* opening.

### The hospital

Exteriors and interiors for the sequence where Michael finds his father unguarded were shot at hospital locations in Manhattan; the specific building is variously attributed and the production used more than one.

## Sicily

The Sicilian sequences were **not** shot in Corleone. The real town had modernized past usefulness by 1971 — too much concrete, too many aerials.

### Savoca

**Bar Vitelli**, in the hill town of Savoca (province of Messina), is where Michael asks Signor Vitelli for [Apollonia's](../characters/apollonia-vitelli.md) hand. It is a real bar, it still operates, and it trades on this fact energetically. There is a plaque and a great deal of merchandise.

The **Church of San Nicolò / Santa Lucia** in Savoca is the church where Michael and Apollonia marry.

### Forza d'Agrò

A few kilometers away, and the trilogy's most-used Sicilian location — it appears in **all three films**. The village square, the church steps, and the surrounding hills carry the Corleone-village scenes, Vito's 1901 flight in *Part II*, and *Part III* material.

Both towns are on the standard Taormina day-trip circuit and are extremely aware of why you have come.

### Palermo

[*Part III*](../films/the-godfather-part-iii-1990.md)'s finale is at the **Teatro Massimo**, Palermo — the largest opera house in Italy and the third largest in Europe. It is a real theatre and the steps are the real steps.

The theatre had been closed for restoration since 1974 and was still shut when the film shot there in 1990; it reopened in 1997. So the most famous steps in the building's modern life were filmed while it was derelict.

Also **Taormina**, for various *Part III* exteriors.

## Nevada and California

**Lake Tahoe** provided the Corleone estate in [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md) — the **Fleur du Lac** estate on the California side, built for Henry Kaiser in the 1930s. Some of the structures used in the film survive; the property is now a private condominium development and is not accessible.

**Las Vegas material was largely shot in California.** The production did not have the budget to shoot the Vegas sequences in Vegas — which is why [Moe Greene's](../characters/moe-greene.md) casino world is thinner than the rest of the film.

The **Woltz estate** — the horse's head — was shot at a Beverly Hills property; the specific attribution is contested between the **Beverly House** on Lexington Road and the **Sands Point Preserve** on Long Island, and different sources give different answers for the interiors and exteriors.

---

## The Dominican Republic

All of [*Part II*](../films/the-godfather-part-ii-1974.md)'s **Havana** was shot in **Santo Domingo**.

Cuba was obviously unavailable in 1973. The Dominican Republic was convenient because Paramount's parent conglomerate, **Gulf+Western** under Charles Bluhdorn, was at that moment aggressively developing the country as a production base and had substantial local infrastructure and political access.

## If you're visiting

**New York.** The Staten Island house and the Foley Square courthouse are the two that hold up. **Arthur Avenue** in the Bronx is worth going to on its own merits and happens to be where the restaurant scene was shot.

**Sicily.** Savoca and Forza d'Agrò are close together and easily done in a day from Taormina. Expect the tourism to be extremely present. The real **Corleone** is a different trip entirely — inland from Palermo, not on any *Godfather* circuit, and home to the **CIDMA** anti-Mafia museum, which is a serious institution and a corrective to everything else on this list.

**Please respect private property.** The Staten Island house, Fleur du Lac, and the Beverly Hills estates are people's homes.
