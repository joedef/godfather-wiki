# Start Here

Three questions people arrive with. Here are the answers.

---

## "Which version do I watch?"

**Watch the theatrical cuts, in release order, first.** *The Godfather* (1972), then *Part II* (1974), then — after a pause, and with adjusted expectations — *Part III* (1990) or its 2020 recut *Coda*.

This is not the obvious answer, because two of the most famous alternate versions rearrange the films chronologically, and chronological sounds like it should be better. It isn't. Here's why:

**Do not start with a chronological recut.** *The Godfather Saga* (1977) and *The Godfather Trilogy: 1901–1980* (1992) reassemble the films into straight chronological order — young Vito's rise first, then the 1945 story, then 1958. They're fascinating objects and worth seeing once. They also destroy the thing the films are actually built on. *Part II*'s power comes entirely from **juxtaposition**: Vito's rise cut directly against Michael's decay, one scene commenting on the next. Straighten the timeline and you have two competent gangster stories instead of one tragedy. See [Structure & Parallel Editing](analysis/structure-and-parallel-editing.md).

**Part III or Coda?** If you've never seen either, watch **[Coda](films/the-godfather-coda-2020.md)** (2020). Coppola's re-edit moves the Vatican-conspiracy setup to the front, trims the fat, and changes the ending. It is a genuinely better-shaped film. If you already know *Part III*, watching Coda is a worthwhile exercise in how much editing alone can do.

**A note on picture quality.** Any release from the 2008 [Coppola Restoration](films/recuts-and-restorations.md) forward is fine. Earlier home video transfers were catastrophically brightened by technicians who thought the films looked "too dark" — the exact opposite of what [Gordon Willis](people/gordon-willis.md) intended. If you can see clearly into Vito's eyes in the opening office scene, you are watching a bad transfer.

## "Should I read the book?"

Yes, but know what you're getting.

Puzo's [novel](books/the-godfather-1969.md) is a fast, lurid, enormously entertaining bestseller with a serious book hiding inside it. Roughly two-thirds of it is the story you know. The other third is Hollywood melodrama, a subplot about a woman's anatomy that has embarrassed readers for fifty years, and a great deal of exposition about Vito's early life that Coppola cut and then, three years later, filmed as half of *Part II*.

The novel is better than its reputation and worse than the film. Read it after the films, not before — the film improves the book's material so consistently that going in the other direction is deflating. [Book vs. Film](analysis/book-vs-film.md) has the scene-by-scene comparison.

The three continuation novels ([Winegardner](books/the-godfather-returns-2004.md) ×2, [Falco](books/the-family-corleone-2012.md)) are for completists. Winegardner in particular is a real novelist doing real work; he is also filling in gaps that arguably didn't need filling.

## A first-time viewer's watchlist of small things

Things that reward attention on a first pass, without spoiling anything structural:

1. **Count the oranges.** They appear before nearly every death or attempted death. Coppola has half-denied it was deliberate; [the pattern is there anyway](analysis/doors-and-thresholds.md#the-oranges).
2. **Watch the light on Michael's face** relative to everyone else's, from the wedding onward. Willis lights him differently as the trilogy proceeds. See [Cinematography](analysis/cinematography.md).
3. **Notice who is allowed in which room.** Doors close on Kay three separate times across two films, and the third one is the last shot of *Part II*'s first half. See [Doors, Thresholds & Frames](analysis/doors-and-thresholds.md).
4. **Listen for the trumpet.** Rota's main theme is almost always a single unaccompanied horn — one voice, no family. See [Music](analysis/music.md).
5. **Track the word "business."** Every atrocity in the trilogy is preceded by somebody calling it business and not personal. Nobody in the films is telling the truth when they say this. See [Themes](analysis/themes.md).
